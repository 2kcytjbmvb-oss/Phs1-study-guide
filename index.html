‚ö° Unit 1: Energy

Energy is the ability to cause a change. It's not a "thing" itself, but a property of objects in a system.

Energy Storage Modes (E-subscript Notation)

+

We use "E-subscript" notation to show HOW energy is stored. Remember: it's all the same energy, just stored differently‚Äîlike money in different bank accounts!

Energy Stored in MOTION:

  • Kinetic Energy (Ek) ‚Äî Energy of a moving object
  • Thermal Energy (Eth) ‚Äî Energy of particle motion (temperature)

Energy Stored in POSITION/ARRANGEMENT:

  • Gravitational Energy (Eg) ‚Äî Position in Earth's gravitational field
  • Elastic Energy (Eel) ‚Äî Stretched/compressed springs
  • Chemical Energy (Ech) ‚Äî Chemical bonds (batteries, food, fuel)
  • Physical State Energy (Eph) ‚Äî Arrangement of particles

Internal Energy (Eint):

Energy stored in changes to structure/temperature that can't be easily recovered (like energy "lost" to friction)

Energy Transfer Mechanisms

+

Energy transfers are PROCESSES (actions), not things. We use the "-ing" form to emphasize they are actions:

  • Working (W) ‚Äî Transfer by pushing/pulling (force √ó distance)
  • Heating (Q) ‚Äî Transfer due to temperature difference (hot ‚Üí cold)
  • Radiating (R) ‚Äî Transfer by electromagnetic waves (light)

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Distinction

A system does NOT "have" heat. A high-temperature system stores energy and can transfer it through the process of heating.

System Schemas & Energy Diagrams

+

System Schema:

  1. Draw circles for each object
  2. Connect with lines to show interactions
  3. Draw boundary around objects IN the system
  4. Objects outside = "surroundings"

Closed System: Energy transfers between modes but stays in system

Open System: Energy can enter or leave

Energy Pie Charts:

  • Each slice = type of energy storage
  • Total pie size = total system energy
  • Closed system ‚Üí all pies same size
  • Energy leaving ‚Üí pies get smaller

Energy Bar Charts (LOL Diagrams):

  • Left bars = Initial energy storage
  • Center = Energy flow in/out of system
  • Right bars = Final energy storage

Conservation of Energy (1st Law of Thermodynamics)

+

Energy cannot be created or destroyed‚Äîonly transferred or converted.

ŒîEsystem = ŒîEg + ŒîEel + ŒîEph + ŒîEch + ŒîEk + ŒîEth
ŒîEsystem = W + Q + R

Initial Energy + Energy In = Final Energy + Energy Out

Instantaneous vs. Average Velocity

+

Average velocity = total displacement / total time (slope of secant line)

Instantaneous velocity = velocity at a specific moment (slope of tangent line)

Finding Instantaneous Velocity from a Curved x-t Graph:

Method 1: Tangent Line

Draw a line that just touches the curve at that point. Find its slope.

Method 2: Surrounding Points

Use points just before and after. Calculate Œîx/Œît for this small interval.

üí° Key Insight

As you shrink the time interval smaller and smaller, the secant line approaches the tangent line. The average velocity over a tiny interval ‚âà instantaneous velocity.

Drawing Force Diagrams (Free Body Diagrams)

+
  1. Represent object as a dot or simple shape
  2. Draw ONLY forces acting ON the object
  3. Draw arrows from center pointing outward
  4. Label each force (Fg, FN, FT, etc.)
  5. Arrow length shows relative magnitude
  6. Use tick marks to show equal forces

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes

  • Including forces the object exerts ON others (wrong!)
  • Including "inertia" as a force (it's NOT a force!)
  • Forgetting the normal force

Balanced Forces = Equilibrium

+

When all forces cancel out:

Œ£F = 0 (net force is zero)
Œ£Fx = 0 and Œ£Fy = 0

Object is either:

  • At rest, OR
  • Moving with constant velocity

Acceleration = 0

üö® INERTIA IS NOT A FORCE! üö®

NEVER include "inertia" in a force diagram!
